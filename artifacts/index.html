Artifacts of Self-Determination: The Raise of the Raizal

Made At:

University of Toronto

Role:

Researcher, Writer, 3D Modeler and Visualization Designer

When:

2017-2018

Project Details:

As part of the final thesis project for the Master of Architecture program at the University of Toronto I created a series of Visual Representations to represent a fictional approach of the creation of a new nation for the Raizal community in the archipelago of San Andres, Providencia and Santa Catalina.

A Brief Background

The Archipelago of San Andres, Providencia and Santa Catalina is situated in the Caribbean Sea, at the maritime border between Nicaragua and Colombia. The archipelago is home of the Raizals — a Creole-speaking people of Afro-Caribbean and British descent — who have occupied the archipelago for centuries.

Historically, the archipelago has been a highly contested territory between nations — most notably between Colombia and Nicaragua — for its territorial waters, which have caused generations of negligence and displacement for the Raizals.

Since the Raizals are citizens of Colombia, the fight between Managua and Bogotá in the last decade over their territory has created a series of inconvenient divisions in the place that they have always known, related to, and understood.

The Raizals have lost their freedom to fish and visit the places that they have gone to for centuries. Although these territorial divisions are merely a series of dotted lines illustrated on maps, they are enforced and affect their freedom.

These debates between nations have ignored the fact that the archipelago is a network of islands, banks, cays, and other atolls that act as one connected system and as such, the Raizals should have the freedom to move around it without restrictions.

As usual, Eric leaves his house at 3:00am to go finishing, because around that time there is a mild wind around the archipelago of San Andres, Providencia and Santa Catalina. He normally doesn’t have to go that far from the shore to find a good shoal, but today he and other fisherman know that they have to follow the fish further north, close to the Quita Sueño and Serrana banks, where they like to go sometimes. On the afternoon of the November 19, 2012, after a long day on the water, Eric overheard other fishermen complaining about the news of some International Court, who decided to rule in favor of Nicaragua against Colombia. Eric was familiar with the dispute, in which Nicaragua claimed sovereignty over some islands and the maritime border with Colombia, right across the archipelago.

The Drawings

As Eric takes a glimpse to the map in the newspaper, which illustrated the new limits, he noticed that Quita Sueño and Serrana are now surrounded by Nicaraguan waters, making them the only two Colombian enclaves. Eric didn’t fully understand what that meant or how exactly that was going to affect their lives. For him, the archipelago is a network of islands, banks, cays, and other atolls that act as one connected system. He has gone to these places since he was a kid and learned from his father how to read the ocean and follow the fish, who don’t understand about borders or politics. A few months after he heard that Patricio, his close friend, has lost his boat to the Nicaraguan army because he crossed the border while finishing. Patricio told Eric how he wanted to get close to the spot they used to go up north. The Nicaraguan coast guards have confiscated his boat because he crossed a line that he could not see or understand.
